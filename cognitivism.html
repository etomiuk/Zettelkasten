Cognitivism

Cognitivism is about using our mental capacities to learn (Ertmer & Newby, 2013). This is different from behaviourism because these capacities are not immediately visible. Memory is a very important aspect of learning, and has been explained by the dual-store model (Lecture 4). The first part of the model is the sensory register, where information coming through the senses is perceived, but not yet processed. Because it its extremely limited capacity of a few seconds, only information we pay attention to will be further processed in working memory (Lecture 4).

Attention

Attention is critical in learning because it allows us to focus on a specific stimuli. Composers exploit features of our attention to create interesting music. Some things that naturally capture attention and how they relate to composition:

  • Intensity: Loud sounds will catch attention. When I think about this one, I always think of one time in orchestra, we played Stravinski's Firebird suite, and there is a slow and soft movement followed by a very loud and intense movement. I wasn't asked to play at the end of the slow movement and rehearsal was getting long so I was starting to lose focus. When we began the next movement, my reaction almost looked like this. We can say that I definitely started paying attention after that! Also, some instruments are naturally louder than others. Composers will play around with dynamics and with which instruments play with which and how many instruments will play at the same time to direct a listener's attention. Another fun example of how composers easily use loudness to capture attention is in Hayden's "Surprise Symphony". Taken from: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lLjwkamp3lI
  • Novelty: To make their pieces interesting, composers often introduce new themes, musical ideas, and melodies in their pieces. They also experiment with new combinations of instruments to create new textures or create new rhythms to add variety.
  • Emotions: Music often represents strong emotions. Classical composers (especially in the Romantic era) are really skilled at eliciting emotions from their music.
  • Personal preference: Music that we like will capture our attention more and will be more memorable.

One thing that is really cool about music is that we can also choose to direct our attention to specific components of a piece. Classical composers might write several melodies at once and you can discover them by directing your attention to a specific instrument for example. When writing music, there is often a melody and an accompaniment. The melody is what we usually pay attention to and the accompaniment is what supports and embellishes the melody. However, one can choose to pay attention to the acccompaniment as well. A piece I recently composed for band exploits the fact that there are different melodies one can listen to and I humbly think that this excerpt is a really cool example of the fact that you can choose which melody you want to hear. I combine four different melodies which all work together. Try to direct your attention to each of these melodies to discover them! If you can read music notation, you can follow the score to make it easier (though melody 4 is a bit harder to hear).

Working Memory

Working memory is the next component of the dual-store model of memory. This is where the information that successfully passed the sensory registers goes and is worked with. The information is worked with and processed. Similar to the sensory register, it has time limits, as well as capacity limits (Lecture 4). The primacy and recency effect explain why we remember certain types of information. The primacy effect says that we remember information that is presented first because it's the first thing we pay attention to and we have more time to process it, while the recency effect says that we remember the last thing that is presented to us because it is what is freshest on our minds. For this reason, I think that it is important that a good composition needs a good introduction and conclusion. That way, the introction captures a listener's attention, and the conclusion will also be one of the last things they will remember of a piece. One of the readings talked about repetition as a way to keep information from slipping away from working memory (Ormrod, Ch. 4). Personally, I use this quite a bit in my compositions. I find it difficult to come up with original ideas for a new piece. Unfortunately, musical ideas always seem to come to me at the worst times (in the shower, when I'm walking my dog, when I'm trying to study for a midterm, etc.), so it's important for me to remember these ideas before I lose them. When an idea comes, I constantly sing it to myself until I can go a write it down somewhere, either on paper or in my music notation software. *talk about how it is easier to remember shorter melodies because of limited capacity of WM?*

Long-Term Memory

The final stage in the duel-processing model is long-term memory, where important information form working memory goes and stays for longer periods of time. For information to get to long-term memory, we need to conect it with information that is already there. Composers have many techniques to make their music memorable.
