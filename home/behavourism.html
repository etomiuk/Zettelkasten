Learning Theories

Behaviourism

According to a behaviourist, one has learned when they display correct behaviour (Lecture 2). This behaviour must be observable, so therefore, cannot depend on our mental processes such as reasoning and memory (Lecture 3). Composers can show that they have learnt through the pieces they write: they can easily display a new behaviour by applying a new composition technique and by showing how their piece sounded better than before.

Behaviourists came up with several theories about conditioning that help explain how one learns: classical conditioning and operant conditioning.

Criticism

Behaviourism cannot explain entirely explain how one learns to compose. Oftentimes, I am inspired by other composers and the way they use certain instruments or chords to create textures and effects, and I don't need a reward or punishment to change my behaviour. Learning to compose also requires using knowledge and memory to learn these patterns (see cognitivism), which would go against behaviourist views that learning is purely visible.
