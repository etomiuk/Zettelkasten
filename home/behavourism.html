How do we learn to compose?

Behaviourism: composition as a way of showing improvement

According to a behaviourist, one has learned when they display correct behaviour (Lecture 2). This behaviour must be observable, so therefore, cannot depend on our mental processes such as reasoning and memory (Lecture 3). Composers can show that they have learnt through the pieces they write: they can easily display a new behaviour by applying a new composition technique and by showing how their piece sounded better than before. ***maybe give an example of one of my pieces, a before and after

*** this sentence might be removed if the connection here is too awkward. For behaviourists, the mechanisms through which one learns is through conditioning. They define two types of conditioning: classical conditioning and operant conditioning.

Criticism

Behaviourism cannot explain entirely explain how one learns to compose. Oftentimes, I am inspired by other composers and the way they use certain instruments or chords to create textures and effects, and I don't need a reward or punishment to change my behaviour. Learning to compose also requires using knowledge and memory to learn these patterns (see cognitivism), which would go against behaviourist views that learning is purely visible.
