Writer's block

Writer's block is a huge struggle for anyone engaged in creative and artistic tasks. Lacking inspiration might put some extraneous cognitive load because you need to now spend some effort thinking about creative ideas before even thinking about how to express these ideas in music. Not being able to begin writing music can lead to a decrease in self-efficacy because you literally cannot begin or complete a given task.

Stepping away from the problem

One way of helping writer's block is incubation (Lecture 12). Incubation is when you take time away from a problem and come back to it later (it can be a few hours, days, weeks, etc.). It might sound counterintuitive to stop doing a task in order to help you continue this task, but this is a way to reset your cognitive load to 0. Taking time away allows you to "empty" your working memory from troubling thoughts or unnecessary knowledge that might prevent you from completing the task (so decresing the extraneous load) so that later, you come back to the problem with a fresh mind with more room in your germain load to solve the task.

This time away from the problem also in a way stops the deep processing of the information you're currently working with because you are no longer focusing your attention on the task. When you come back later to solve the problem, during the time away, your brain would have probably made more connections with the experiences you've lived in that time (perhaps unconsciously!) which can help you once you are ready to pay attention to the problem.

The simplest form of incubation is sleep. There is evidence that shows that sleep or even awake quiescence can foster insight for a solution to a problem (Craig et al., 2018). Furthermore, Lewis et al. (2018) argue that the cycles of nREM and REM (REM sleep is where dreams are more likely to occus) are times when the brain abstracts information from memories and creates new connections between them, which is fuel for creative thoughts. Maybe that could explain why so many creative individuals might get their ideas from their dreams!

Reach out to mentors

Another potential solution could be to reach out to a mentor who can provide help in generating ideas based on their expertise. In this process, you might learn how someone else might solve the current problem you're faced with which might help form deeper connections between concepts that you weren't able to make yourself, as well as spark new creative ideas. After all, learning is very social!

In composition this can also be done indirectly by going to listen to some of your favourite pieces. This can help learn how famous composers write certain passages that might be similar to what you are trying to write.

Getting inspiration from AI

As described here, there exist many AI-driven tools that can facilitate composition. Nowadays, we have access to ChatGPT to help us with literally any task. One advantage of AIs such as ChatGPT is that they can help us get started on an idea or a a problem we are stuck on by generating new ideas (Lecture 11). So can ChatGPT help composers when they are stuck in a writer's block? I conducted a few experiments to find out!
