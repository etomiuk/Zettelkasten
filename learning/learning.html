What is learning?

Learning is change (Lecture 2). Different learning theories have varying opinions on how this change is defined. For instance, this change can be in our behaviors, our mental states, our interactions with others and the environment, our knowledge base or our individual needs. Often times, learning involves an improvement of a certain skill or knowledge.

In my experience composing, I can personally track my learning and improvement in my compositions by comparing the complexity of my previous works with my current ones. This can be complexity in terms of how detailed the melody or accompaniement are, what kinds of effects I am able to create, the complexity of the rhythms, etc. Below is a snippet of a piano melody I came up with in high school. It is quite simple since the accompaniment is exactly the same throughout all the measures, and there are not tonality or dynamic contrasts.

In comparison, this is a part of a piece I wrote two years ago. The accompaniment is much more complex since there is a sense of an upwards progression in the left hand for the first 6 measures (notice the first notes of each slurred group of triplets are E, F#, G, C). I also use 3 against 2 polyrhythm in the right hand, which plays eighth notes, and the left hand which plays triplets. I have also learnt the importance of articulations, dynamics and expression markings which help me convey emotions more effectively and make the piece appear more put together visually.

Of course, there is no one learning theory that explanins how someone would learn to compose. Rather, they give a different perspective on different ways learning can be viewed. How would each learning theory analyze how I have learnt to compose? Read on to find out!
