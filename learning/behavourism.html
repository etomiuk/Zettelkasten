Behaviourism: learning is a change in behaviour

According to behaviourism, one has learned when they display correct behaviour (Lecture 3). This behaviour must be observable, so therefore, cannot depend on our mental processes like reasoning and memory (Lecture 4). Composers can show that they have learnt through the pieces they write. They can easily display a new behaviour by applying a new composition technique and comparing how the piece sounded before and after this change. In the pieces I compared here, we can clearly see an improvement in how I write pieces for piano.

How does one learn to change their behaviour? Behaviourists propose two mechanisms: classical and operant conditioning.

Classical conditioning: generating emotions

The goal of classical conditioning is to elicit a certain behaviour, called conditioned response, in response to a certain stimulus, called conditioned stimulus (Lecture 3). The way this is done is that we continuously pair a stimulus that naturally produces the desired behaviour (called unconditioned response) with the stimulus we'd like to cause the behaviour (a neutral stimulus) until the neutral stimulus generates the conditioned response, at which point it becomes the conditioned stimulus. Classical conditioning can explain how we have certain emotional reactions to specific kinds of music, and movie music composers take advantage of this to learn to generate strong emotions in their listeners.

Operant conditioning: learning through a composer's emotions

On the other hand, operant conditioning relies on how behaviour changes after someone has received a consequence. This theory is based on the idea that we can change behaviour if we provide rewards or punishments after a behaviour is elicited (Lecture 3). A lot of our learning is connected to our emotions, so reinforcements could be positive emotions such as happiness, pride, excitement, whereas punishments can be negative emotions we wish to suppress, like anger, sadness, or fear. In this sense, the sound of certain notes or chords could act like a stimulus that causes reinforcement or punishment. Also, someone's reaction to your piece can also serve as a reinforcer. If someone likes your piece, they will give good feedback (positive reinforcement), so you are more likely to repeat the things you used to compose that piece.

In my experience, as a self-taught composer who has little theoretical background on music, I depend a lot on experimentation to figure out the kind of sound I am looking for. When certain chords or melodies sound bad, I am less likely to use those chords. These nasty feeling I get when listening to my bad chords or melodies can be like a positive punishment, since I will never want to use that chord progression or melody again because I feel feelings I do not enjoy. An example from my own work is the following accompaniment to a nice bassoon melody I came up with this summer, in which I personally think that the D# in the violas sounds awkward and muddy. Therefore, because this sounds bad, I will be less likely to produce music that sounds like this to avoid having to hear music like this again.

On the other hand, certain effects I think sound good tend to appear a lot in my pieces. For instance, I tend to write a certain rhythmic pattern quite often in my pieces because I enjoy the feeling of the offbeats that is created with that rhythm. The chills I get when I listen to their sound is like a positive reinforcement that makes me more likely to use them in my pieces. Behaviourism cannot entirely explain how one learns to compose because learning often also involves processes that may not be visible, such as forming deep connections between real life experiences and the content we try to learn (Lecture 5). Oftentimes, I am inspired by other composers and the way they use certain instruments or chords to create textures and effects so I learn from others without necessarily being rewarded or punished. Furthermore, other things, such as motivation can cause changes in visible behaviour, without having the need to add or remove some kind of stimulus after a certain behaviour occurs. However, the consequences that follow a behaviour can then have an influence on our motivation which can help strengthen or weaken this behaviour afterwards in this feedback loop, as described here.
