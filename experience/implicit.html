Previous knowledge

***combine here the stuff about implicit knowledge --> or link to culture?

The previous knowledge we have influences a task we learn, whether this knowledge is related to the task or not. Sometimes, this knowledge can help us learn a new task easily, which is called a positive transfer, and sometimes, it can even hinder and interfere with our learning, which is a negative transfer (Lecture 9). It may also be possible that this knowledge may not have an effect on the new thing we try to learn, which is a zero transfer, but that is more rare (Lecture 9).

If our previous knowledge and skills are related to the new task we are learning, this is called a near transfer, since the transfer of knowledge and learning can easily connect. This type of transfer is a lot easier to do compared to a far transfer, where we are transferring knowledge from a very different domain. When learning to compose, it is evident that previous musical experience can help when writing a piece. By being exposed to music, you implicitly learn some patterns, such as what chords sound like, what instruments sound like, what notes fit in a certain key signature, the beat of a song, etc. (connect to implicit learning). This knowledge can make it easier for you to learn to compose a song. I have no music theory background, but because I have been playing violin for a very long time, I have implicitly acquired knowledge (that I can't really describe becuase it is implicit) about how to make my piece sound good. Sometimes, I "just know" how to write a certain passage based on my implicit knowledge because I get to writing it on paper, whereas someone with little musical experience might struggle more to even come up with a good sounding melody. Writing music for large ensembles comes more easily to me as well because I also have lots of experience playing in orchestras and bands so I get to know how different instruments are used and how they can be combined. Somone who is more used to playing alone might be more overwhelmed with having to write a piece that uses many instruments.

However, as a classical musician, I don't think that I would be very good at composing a jazz piece because it is quite hard to transfer my knowledge about classical composition to jazz. Music basics surely can help (such as idetifying notes and rhythms), but I know that the rhythms and chords used in jazz are quite different. This might be an instance where my classical music knowledge might be a negative transfer to learning to compose a jazz piece, because if I were asked to write a jazz piece, it might end up sounding like something classical because that is the only knowledge I have.
